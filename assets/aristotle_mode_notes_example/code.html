Chapter 12: General Relativity

Albert Einstein's General Theory of Relativity, presented in 1915, is a cornerstone of modern physics. It describes gravity not as a force, but as a consequence of the curvature of spacetime caused by the uneven distribution of mass and energy.

Spacetime and Gravity

In classical mechanics, space and time are static, absolute entities. Relativity unifies them into a single four-dimensional continuum: spacetime. The presence of matter and energy curves this fabric. Objects follow the straightest possible path through this curved spacetime, which we perceive as the force of gravity. A useful analogy is a bowling ball (a massive object) placed on a stretched rubber sheet (spacetime), causing it to dimple. A marble (a smaller object) rolled nearby will curve towards the bowling ball, not because of a direct pull, but because it is following the curvature of the sheet.

Einstein Field Equations

The relationship between spacetime curvature and the distribution of matter and energy is described by the Einstein Field Equations. A simplified representation is:

Gμν = 8πG Tμν

Here, Gμν represents the geometry of spacetime (the Einstein tensor), and Tμν represents the stress-energy tensor of matter and radiation. The constant G is Newton's gravitational constant. These equations show that the distribution of energy and momentum determines the geometry of spacetime.

Experimental Evidence

The theory has been confirmed by numerous observations:

  • Perihelion precession of Mercury: General Relativity accurately predicts the small discrepancy in the orbit of Mercury that couldn't be explained by Newtonian physics.
  • Deflection of starlight: The theory predicted that light from distant stars would be bent by the Sun's gravity. This was famously confirmed by Sir Arthur Eddington's expedition during the 1919 solar eclipse.

Analysis of General Relativity

The General Theory of Relativity, formulated by Albert Einstein in 1915, provides a unified description of gravity as a geometric property of spacetime.

Fundamental Principles

The theory is founded upon the Principle of Equivalence, which posits that the effects of gravitation are indistinguishable from the effects of acceleration.

"An observer... cannot distinguish between the effects of being in a uniform gravitational field and being in a uniformly accelerating reference frame."

Mathematical Formulation

The core of General Relativity is encapsulated in the Einstein Field Equations (EFE).

Rμν - ½ R gμν = (8πG / c⁴) Tμν

A logical breakdown:

  • Left-hand side (Geometry): Represents the curvature of spacetime, described by the Einstein tensor (Gμν).
  • Right-hand side (Matter/Energy): Represents the distribution of matter and energy, described by the stress-energy tensor (Tμν).
